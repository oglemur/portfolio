# Copy & Case Study Guide

This file tells Claude how to work with Christof on writing portfolio copy — case studies, project descriptions, and any other narrative content on this site.

---

## The voice

Christof's copy is casual, specific, and self-aware. It reads like a smart person talking, not a PM resume.

**Good:**
> "I was 2. Traded my pacifier to start my dad's motorcycle. Worth it."
> "446 bridges, 3 rivers, and the MHCI program. The 412 has the smartest people I know."
> "Boarding school. Picked up Afrikaans, fell in love with braai. No regrets."

**Bad:**
> "Leveraged cross-functional collaboration to deliver a scalable, user-centered solution."
> "This project allowed me to explore the intersection of technology and human experience."
> "As the lead designer, I was responsible for end-to-end ownership of the design process."

**Rules:**
- Short sentences over long ones. Break at natural beats.
- Specific over general. A real detail beats a polished abstraction.
- First person, but not self-congratulatory.
- Humor is fine. Earnestness is fine. Corporate polish is not.
- Never use: "leverage", "synergy", "user-centered", "end-to-end", "stakeholders" (unless quoting someone else), "scalable" as a compliment.
- Contractions are fine. Incomplete sentences are fine.

## Case study structure

Every case study follows this arc — but not every section needs equal weight:

1. **Brief** — What was the ask? Who asked? What was the context?
2. **Problem** — What was the actual tension being resolved? If there's a good one-liner, make it a blockquote.
3. **Research** — What did you learn? Lead with the insight, not the methodology. Quotes from users are gold.
4. **Solution** — What did you build? Describe it plainly first, then explain why the specific decisions matter.
5. **Key moments** — The interesting design decisions. What changed, what you fought for, what surprised you.
6. **Outcome** — What happened? Numbers if you have them. Reception if you don't.
7. **Reflection** — One honest thing you'd do differently, or what the constraint taught you. No fake humility.

Not every project has all seven. Some have two or three that matter and the rest is filler — cut the filler.

## How to work with Christof on copy

### Starting a new case study

1. If Christof has an old version somewhere (Webflow, Notion, doc), fetch it first. It's the raw material, not the final copy.
2. Read it, then identify: what's the strongest 20%? What's the weakest 20% that should be cut entirely?
3. Don't transplant old copy. Rewrite it in the voice above, using the old content as source material.

### Gathering new content

Ask questions one section or city at a time — not all at once. Use the pattern from the city blurbs session:
- Give context about what you're asking and why
- Ask 2–4 specific questions, not a generic "tell me about this project"
- Listen for the specific detail (the pacifier, the braai, the 412) — that's what becomes the copy
- Draft, then ask for corrections, don't just ask them to write it themselves

### Good questions to ask per section

**Brief / context:**
- Who set this brief — client, course, both?
- What were you actually trying to do, in one sentence?

**Problem:**
- What was broken or missing before you started?
- Is there a stat or number that captures the stakes?

**Research:**
- What did you learn that surprised you or changed your direction?
- Any direct quotes from users worth keeping?

**Solution:**
- What does it actually do, in plain English?
- What's the one decision you're most proud of?
- What did you cut that you wish you'd kept?

**Outcome:**
- How was it received?
- Any numbers? (time saved, users, scores, funding, awards)

**Reflection:**
- What would you do differently?
- What did the constraint teach you?

## Condensing old case studies

Old Webflow portfolio copy tends to be too long and too formal. When condensing:

- Cut all methodology sections that read like a UX textbook ("We conducted affinity mapping and thematic analysis...")
- Keep any direct user quotes
- Keep specific numbers and stats
- Keep the unusual or personal details (the HoloLens, the cardboard prototype, the 12 test participants)
- Remove anything that sounds like it's justifying the process rather than describing the outcome
- Target: each section should be 1–3 sentences. If it's longer, ask if it needs to be.

---

## Visuals

When images are available (from old portfolio, Figma, etc.):
- Use them. A good visual replaces 3 sentences of description.
- Caption format: lowercase, descriptive, plain. E.g. `"status bar · locked (riding)"` not `"Figure 3: The Status Bar Component in Locked State"`
- GIFs for interactions, stills for layouts and structure
- Don't describe what's already visible in an image
